This presentation will be about illegal numbers.

Those are numbers whose very possession is illegal, which—despite sounding very silly—is a very real issue, especially in modern times.

How, you may be asking?

Well, let's imagine you somehow learned the Gold Codes (the codes allowing to launch a nuclear strike).

Like, overhear them somehow, not steal them. That would be without question illegal.

You're not supposed to have that knowledge, and it's dangerous! Is it illegal?

Let's see a more realistic example.

First, I will explain the AACS DRM system quickly:

  • company pays to get a decryption key,
  • key gets put somewhere in playback software,
  • which can then decrypt AACS-protected content.

Thing is, while looking for the key in the software is hard and punishable,

playing back videos in some softwares just yields the key in memory!

Since no reverse-engineering was done, it's disputable whether this breaks the law.

One key (and others later) was shared around and included in some unlicensed software, such as DeCSS.

Thing is, the AACS LA wasn't too happy about this, and sent DMCA takedown notices to various sites.

People actually fought back, and started figuring out ways to embed the key into other types of data, that would be harder to take down.

So, getting back to that flag we saw previously...

This flag's colors encode the key's bytes, plus one extra byte at the end.

Concatenate all the bytes and you have the key!

The trick is, transforming the number into an image gives it a more "archivable" quality.

This picture went more and more viral as the AACS Licensing Authority tried taking it down

This kind of phenomenon is called a Streisand Effect, named after Barbara Streisand, who tried to get pictures of her manor removed from the Internet, and achieved the complete opposite.

But can we do better?

Of course we can. This is just encoding the key, but that is a number—it's no fun.

The source code for the decoding program was to be taken down because it decoded AACS without a license.

Back to numbers: a file is just bytes, which is numbers, right? Like the key before...

Take the source code to the program.

There are several gzip files that will decompress to that exact program, you just need to add padding bytes at the end.

Thus, several numbers that correspond to a gzipped DeCSS source!

Math people like prime numbers. Especially extra large.

Let's just find a prime number that "decompresses" to the source code, and get it published!

And so we now also have illegal primes.

This one in the background actually encodes an executable version of DeCSS. Not even compressed!

The thing is, some of these prime numbers were large enough that they deserved to be archived if only for that reason.

And since it's an intrinsic property, they can't just be "deleted"..!

Naturally, the AACS LA wasn't too happy with this, and looked into ways to have everything deleted.

One of the last comments they gave, regarding the flood of copies, encoded or not, of the key, was that things took an interesting new twist.

Another class of illegal numbers is numbers associated with something illegal.

For example, the number 88 is associated to Nazism, because H is the 8th letter of the alphabet, and 88 => HH => Heil Hitler.

Let's back up a bit.

We have seen that some numbers can be illegal (or at least arguably so), but maybe we should take a moment to consider the implications?

Reusing that last example of illegal number, what if a computer system automatically assigned product ID #88 to some pesticide, that's just a honest mistake.

But then on the other hand there is information that should really really really stay secret (recall the nuclear launch codes).

And in the middle, we have things like DRM keys.

This tends to lean into the debate of whether DRM should exist or not, the question of intellectual property.

In the end, maybe we're all overthinking it too much. It's just numbers, right?

Why do we give some numbers so much meaning?

Why are some numbers so important to us?
