What can make programming unfun?

Let's get to what you came here for! Since we put ‚Äúrediscovering‚Äù in the talk's title, let's explore why we might have lost that fun. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Barriers to entry

  • Waiting for long compilation
  • Getting a bunch of dependencies
  • Needing more powerful hardware
The first thing that drains fun is being taken out of ‚Äúthe zone‚Äù to deal with some chore. ‚è≠Ô∏è

For example, waiting for long build times. Stressing your network bandwidth (and waiting some more) while it fetches a whole bunch of libraries. ‚è≠Ô∏è

And hardware requirements constantly increasing. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Hard to finish projects this complex

Modern game development techniques tend to demand a lot out of many specialized people, so it can be barely possible to actually finish something. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Hard to wrap your head around

  • Many different targets
  • And constantly evolving, too!
  • Debugging suffers
  • Grasping the overall functioning feels nice
The modern PC platform is very diverse, with a lot of screen sizes, hardware combinations and configurations... ‚è≠Ô∏è

And each of those targets are moving, to make things worse! ‚è≠Ô∏è

This especially hinders debugging, since that necessitates fairly broad understanding of the systems you're debugging ‚è≠Ô∏è

And, finally, the sensation that things are getting beyond your control just doesn't feel great. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Sure, you can make modern development that simple too. Write some assembly or C, compile it for your computer (probably x86 or ARM), and run; no libraries, no abstractions, no layers. But it's very difficult to be prroductive or have fun that way.

You, the platform, and nothing in-between

  • Enables small team sizes

  • Constraining
    • Limitations breed creativity

One dev (or a small team) can wear more hats without any one of them being too big to handle.

The various kinds of Game Boy development

This niche has a little something for everybody!

Creating games is, of course, a central part of Game Boy development, but it's not the only one. From tinkering with the hardware itself, to creating pixel art and music not meant for a game, to writing tools for the Game Boy on other platforms, there's a little something for everybody!

Pixel art

  • Dedicated pixel art tools (Aseprite, Piskel...)
  • DMG art (4 shades of gray, dithering for nuance)
  • GBC art (unusual colour restrictions)
You can use practically any graphics editor to draw things one pixel at a time. But the Game Boy has more restrictions than that. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Dedicated editors for pixel art have useful features, like working with limited color palettes or animating a frame at a time. ‚è≠Ô∏è

The original Game Boy (code name "Dot Matrix Game", or DMG) could show exactly four shades or gray (or a low-contrast green on real hardware). But even then, you can use dithering -- varying colors over space, or even over time -- to look like you have more. ‚è≠Ô∏è

The Game Boy Color supported 32 thousand whole colors -- but still with its own limits around how they're divided into palettes and tiles. But once again, you can use artistic and programming trickery to create "high-color" cutscenes or demos.
