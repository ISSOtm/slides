What can make programming unfun?

Let's get to what you came here for! Since we put ‚Äú**re**discovering‚Äù in the talk's title, let's explore why we might have lost that fun. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Barriers to entry

  • Waiting for long compilation
  • Getting a bunch of dependencies
  • Needing more powerful hardware
The first thing that drains fun is being taken out of ‚Äúthe zone‚Äù to deal with some chore. ‚è≠Ô∏è

For example, waiting for long build times. Raise your hand if you have seen that comic somewhere before? ‚è≠Ô∏è

There's also stressing your network bandwidth (and waiting some more) while you are fetching a whole bunch of libraries... ‚è≠Ô∏è

And hardware requirements constantly increasing. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Hard to finish projects this complex

Modern game development techniques tend to demand a lot out of a single person, so it can be discouraging to actually *finish* something. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Hard to wrap your head around

Speaking of complexity, the raw complexity of the systems also makes it difficult to grasp what's going on ‚è≠Ô∏è

I mean, what are you supposed to do when your rendering pipeline is *this* complex? ‚è≠Ô∏è

Hard to wrap your head around

  • Many different targets
  • And constantly evolving, too!
  • Debugging suffers
  • Grasping the overall functioning feels nice
The modern PC platform is very diverse, with a lot of screen sizes, hardware combinations and configurations... ‚è≠Ô∏è

And each of those targets are moving, to make things worse! ‚è≠Ô∏è

This especially hinders debugging, since that necessitates fairly broad understanding of the systems you're debugging ‚è≠Ô∏è

And, finally, the sensation that things are escaping you just *doesn't feel great* ‚è≠Ô∏è

The various kinds of Game Boy development

This niche has a little something for everybody!

Programming games

  • Assembly
  • C
  • Niche languages (Wiz, Millfork, TRSE...)
  • Not C++ nor Rust (yet)
  • Python, JS...
If you want to program games, there is a variety of languages available: ‚è≠Ô∏è

The first, the most historical and original one, is assembly; which, in a nutshell, is you directly talking to the CPU. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Then there's the usual suspect, C; though it's poorly suited for the Game Boy, at least currently ‚è≠Ô∏è

Thus, some languages have been created to fill the gap, and be a middle-ground between assembly's no-frills and C's awkwardness. I'd be surprised if you've ever heard of any of them before, but they're nonetheless worth giving a try! ‚è≠Ô∏è

You might expect C++ and Rust to be available, like C; unfortunately, that is ‚è≠Ô∏è currently not the case. There are ongoing efforts to change that, though. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Some languages, typically the dynamically-typed, memory-GC'd languages, have essentially no hope of ever running on the console. There's been toy implementations (for example Micropython), but realistically, they offer nowhere near the Python anyone is used to. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Another interesting field is the world of emulation. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Now, some people write emulators *on* the Game Boy, but due to the system's low specs, the opportunities are rare. ‚è≠Ô∏è

As for emulating the Game Boy itself, it's a common recommendation for newbies, since getting it mostly right is not particularly difficult (unlike, say, the NES) ‚è≠Ô∏è

That said, if you like challenge, the Game Boy has a *lot* of headaches in store for you! Here are two of the more famous expert-level targets, but don't worry, there's *plenty* more where that came from :) ‚è≠Ô∏è

Overall, it can a fun activity, because the system has been researched a lot *and* said research is publicly available; and there are a lot of people who will readily give you a hand. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Research

  • Running tests on hardware, and making them reproducible
  • Documentation (Pan Docs...)
  • Again, can be done as a community effort
Although we just said that the system has been studied a lot, believe it or not, research on that 35-yo system is STILL ongoing! ‚è≠Ô∏è

One notable area of work is writing what we call ‚Äútest ROMs‚Äù, which enable individual behaviours to be compared between implementations (including the, yknow, reference one :P) ‚è≠Ô∏è

And another thing that anyone, yes *you*, can help with, is documenting all of the technical details in a more human-friendly and accessible manner. ‚è≠Ô∏è

And all of this is also something that other people will gladly help you with! ‚è≠Ô∏è
