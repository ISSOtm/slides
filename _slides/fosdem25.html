What can make programming unfun?

üê∂ Let's get to what you came here for! Since we put ‚Äúrediscovering‚Äù in the talk's title, let's discuss what can be more fun about retro development. Where did the fun go?

Barriers to entry

  • Waiting for long compilation
  • Getting a bunch of dependencies
  • Needing more powerful hardware
üê± The first thing that drains fun is being taken out of ‚Äúthe zone‚Äù to deal with some chore. ‚è≠Ô∏è

For example, waiting for long build times. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Stressing your network bandwidth (and waiting some more) while it fetches a whole bunch of libraries. ‚è≠Ô∏è

And hardware requirements constantly increasing.

Hard to finish complex projects

üê∂ Modern development techniques tend to demand a lot out of many specialized people, so it can be barely possible to actually finish something, especially as a solo creator.

Hard to wrap your head around

  • Many different targets
  • And constantly evolving, too!
  • Debugging suffers
  • Grasping the overall functioning feels nice
üê± It's also just hard, man! ‚è≠Ô∏è

The modern PC platform is very diverse, with a lot of screen sizes, hardware combinations and configurations... ‚è≠Ô∏è

And each of those targets are moving, to make things worse! ‚è≠Ô∏è

üê∂ This especially hinders debugging, since that necessitates fairly broad understanding of the systems you're debugging ‚è≠Ô∏è

And, finally, the sensation that things are getting beyond your control just doesn't feel great.

Why going back to older times fixes those

Developing for a 90's console doesn't mean you must go back to a 90's experience

üê± So, now, let's show why retro game dev can be a solution to all of the above. ‚è≠Ô∏è

"Developing for a 90's console doesn't mean you must go back to a 90's experience."

Modern tools for a retro platform

  1. Create: graphics editors, music sequencers
  2. Build: assemblers, compilers
  3. Run: emulators, debuggers
üê∂ The Game Boy's tools, SDKs, libraries, and available resources have improved a lot over the decades. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Even pixel art and four-channel music can benefit from the latest programs for creating them (no more plotting pixels on graph paper!). ‚è≠Ô∏è

You can compile all your source code and graphics into a playable ROM in mere seconds. ‚è≠Ô∏è

And you can use modern debugging conveniences, like runtime breakpoints and time-travel debugging, all within your emulator.

It's just less complex

  • Good concept-learning material...
  • ...but still fun and engaging on its own
  • Enables small team sizes
  • Baseline expectations are much easier to meet
  • Constraining
    • Limitations breed creativity
    • Sometimes feels like a puzzle-solving activity
üê± The Game Boy's simplicity is refreshing in many ways ‚è≠Ô∏è

Not only is it a good support to learn some fundamental lower-level concepts, ‚è≠Ô∏è

it remains an interesting object in its own right! ‚è≠Ô∏è

Further, one dev (or a small team) can wear more hats at once, without any one of them being too big to handle. ‚è≠Ô∏è

This is, in part, due to the system's lower specs making it easier to have something that feels reasonably complete. ‚è≠Ô∏è

And, yes, the system is under-powered, but that's a *good thing* ‚è≠Ô∏è

as the saying goes: ‚Äúlimitations breed creativity‚Äù. ‚è≠Ô∏è

It can also make it really interesting to have to solve minor challenges to make your ideas work on the system

Pixel art

  • Dedicated pixel art tools (Aseprite, Piskel...)
  • Monochrome GB art (4 shades of gray, dithering for nuance)
  • GBC art (unusual colour restrictions)
üê∂ You can use practically any graphics editor to draw things one pixel at a time. But the Game Boy has more restrictions than that. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Dedicated editors for pixel art have useful features, like working with limited color palettes or animating a frame at a time. ‚è≠Ô∏è

The original Game Boy could show exactly four shades its famous ‚Äúpea soup green‚Äù. But even then, you can use dithering -- mixing colors over space, or even ‚è≠Ô∏è over time -- to look like you have more. ‚è≠Ô∏è

The Game Boy Color supported 32 thousand whole colors -- but still with its own limits around how they're divided into palettes and tiles. But once again, you can use artistic and programming trickery to create "high-color" cutscenes or demos.

  • Assemblers, compilers
  • Graphics editors, mapping tools, audio sequencers

üê± Feel like doing some work outside of the console? We got you covered! ‚è≠Ô∏è

üê∂ More on the GUI side, there are tools for creating your game assets -- graphics editors, tilemap editors, audio sequencers, etc. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Individual games may also use their own custom tools like for scripting cutscenes, or defining enemy AI; really, your imagination is the limit. ‚è≠Ô∏è

If you'd rather not make your own game and your own tools for it, there are several GBDev projects that are looking for help right now. There is a strong culture of free software, so anyone can contribute (and even get paid a little as thanks).

Emulators

  • 80% of games are easy to accurately emulate
  • Want difficulty? Try emulating Prehistorik Man pixel-perfectly... or Pinball Deluxe at all!

  • Well-understood (mostly), well-documented, a lot of people can help
üê∂ Another interesting field is the world of emulation. ‚è≠Ô∏è

Research

  • Running tests on hardware, and making them reproducible
  • Documentation (Pan Docs...)
  • Again, can be done as a community effort
üê± Although we just said that the system has been studied a lot, believe it or not, research on that 35-yo system is STILL ongoing! ‚è≠Ô∏è

One notable area of work is writing what we call ‚Äútest ROMs‚Äù, which enable individual behaviours to be compared between implementations (including the, y'know, original physical one :P) ‚è≠Ô∏è
